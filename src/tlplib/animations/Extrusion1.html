  • Pellet feed in made on a large scale at a crude oil processing plant. This makes it cheap and readily available.
  • Pellet feed, due to its more consistent particle size, will have large gaps when in the hopper. This makes adding & mixing dopants or other additives is difficult.

  • Powder feed takes more energy to manufacture due to its smaller dimensions.
  • Powder feed has a large range of particle sizes, allowing it to fill most gaps between its larger grains.
  • Dopants and additives are much easier to mix in as they can disperse well and won't aggregate together to fall through gaps between the powder grains.

    • In flood feeding, the feed is unconstrained in how much can flow into the extruder.
    • This is good for maximising throughput, as the extruder is working at maximum capacity.
    • The main drawbacks of this method include:
      • The inability to vary the screw speed,
      • Higher liklihood of agglomerates forming.
    • In starve feeding, the feed is metered into the extruder with a feeder, and the bottom part of the hopper is not completely full. This limits the maximum throughput of the extruder.
    • There are some considerable advantages to using starve feeding:
      • Firstly, the screw speed can be varied at constant output, as well as the output can be varied at constant screw speed.
      • Melting action is faster since the pellets are not compacted into a dense solid bed,
      • Mixing action may be improved.

  • Plastic feed can take the form ofor.
  • The feed enters the extruder through the hopper.
  • Extruders can be either flood fed or starve fed.

    • The hopper allows the feed to fill this portion of the extruder around the screw.
    • Due to the capacity of the extruder constantly being at maximum capacity, there will be
      one rotation speed setting that optmises between:
      • Complete melting of the polymer before it reaches the nozzle,
      • Minimising the mean time that the polymer spends in the extruder.
    • The hopper meters the feed into the extruder by rotation of a feeder. This can vary the
      input rate (and thus output rate too) by changing its rotation speed.
    • Since the extruder is not working at maximum capacity, the rotation speed of the screw
      can be varied independantly of the feeder.
      • This aids mixing of the polymer.
      • It can also create a more homogenous melt since the molten plastic is more free to flow.
    • This functions of this portion of the extruder are:
      • To allow the feed to enter the extruder and ensure it moves away from the entrance.
      • To keep the feed at a cool/ambient temperature so that the opening can't become sealed by resolidified
        molten polymer.

          
    • This functions of this portion of the extruder are:
      • To start heating the feed up to and past its melting temperature, Tm.
      • To start compressing the feed to aid it in becoming one molten mass.
    • The widening of the screw lowers the available volume for the feed to occupy. This spreads it out and ensures that it merges with the feed from the grooves in front and behind.
    • Alternatively, the screw may not widen, as is shown in the diagram, some simply increase the amount of grooves in the screw gradually, and some specialised ones have both.

                 
    • The functions of this portion of the extruder are:
      • To ensure the polymer
        is all molten and well mixed.
      • To force the polymer
        into the nozzle area by maintaining a high pressure.
    • The wider screw lowers available space, driving the polymer towards the nozzle while lowering its residence time and increasing efficiency, and also maintaining a high pressure for a consistent extrudate.

                 
    • The nozzle controls the shape of the final extrudate.
